Mission Summary Luch 2:
Russian military SIGINT satellite LUCH (OLYMP-K) 2 (2023-031A) was launched  on 12 March 2023, and like its predecessor LUCH (OLYMP-K) 1, it has since moved about along the geostationary belt, doing proximity operations near western commercial geostationary satellites. 

Each two to four months, it is moved to a new target, where it stays in close proximity until moved to a new target again, behaviour in a similar fashion to the US satellite PAN/NEMESIS-1 in the past. It is probably clandestinely accessing and monitoring communication streams of the target satellites, and perhaps mapping contact networks. It can sometimes come very close to its target, to within a few to a few tens of kilometers.

From September 2024 onwards it was positioned at 0.93 W close to Intelsat 1002, but on February 7, 2025, it started a drift eastwards. After a significant one-month drift over 62 degrees in longitude, it settled again on March 7  near 62 E, its new eavesdropping target being Intelsat 39 (2029-049B). This latest move is the largest since it was launched.

Mission Summary Intelsat 39:
Intelsat 39 is designed with wide and high-powered steerable spot beams and replaced Intelsat 902 at 62° East to meet the needs of broadband networking, video, and government customers across Africa, Asia-Pacific, Europe, and the Middle East. It provides C- and Ku-band capabilities and enhanced mobile connectivity for aeronautical, maritime, and government users operating in the Indian Ocean region. It provides a powerful platform enabling mobile network operators, enterprises, and internet service providers to deliver faster and more efficient connectivity services, and gives governments the ability to expand connectivity to remote areas and narrow the digital divide.

The German Bundeswehr (armed forces) is among the known users of Intelsat 39. Both Intelsat 10-02 and Intelsat 39 provide C-band and Ku-band coverage across Europe and the Middle East, including Ukraine. 

According to reporting, the satellite's proximity allows Russia to intercept communications sent between Earth and Intelsat-39 because those transmissions are not encrypted. European military representatives and security agencies reportedly use such civilian satellites at least in part because secure alternatives remain limited. Experts also believe Luch-2 may be able to intercept control signals sent from ground stations to satellites such as Intelsat-39, raising the possibility of operational interference.
